Your Community Guide 💛

For Soulmates, new members, carers, and supporters

Safe Soulmates is a warm, welcoming community for neurodivergent and disabled adults. We support friendships, confidence, skills, volunteering, training, and belonging. Everyone deserves to feel safe, heard, and included here.

The things in this guide aren't meant to feel harsh or like a list of "don'ts." They come from our own members, who told us what genuinely helps them navigate the wonderful, but sometimes socially exhausting, world of "peopling." Think of this as wisdom from people who get it. 💜

💜 Friendships and relationships

Everyone joins for friendship and socials onlySafe Soulmates is, first and foremost, a friendship community. We are honest from the very beginning: we cannot meet everyone who joins looking for a relationship, and that is something we want you to know from day one. Not everyone will want a romantic relationship — and that is completely fine. Friendships are just as important and just as valued here.

Slow connections, not bombardmentA lot of people want connection to grow slowly, starting as friendship. Nobody likes being bombarded with messages or attention, especially early on. Take things at a gentle pace, give people space, and let any connection grow naturally over time.

Strong feelings early on — what's happeningSometimes our brains can create very intense feelings for someone quite quickly, especially when we're excited or hopeful — this is sometimes called limerence. It can feel real and overwhelming, but it isn't the same as truly knowing someone. It's OK to notice this in yourself and take a step back. Getting to know someone slowly, as a friend, gives any connection the best chance to be real.

🤝 Being honest with you

Safe Soulmates is not a dating agency. We cannot guarantee romantic relationships. Like many communities, our membership isn't perfectly balanced, which means matching is not always possible for everyone, no matter their gender or who they're interested in.

We will never raise your hopes unfairly. What we can promise is that we will always treat you with honesty, care, and respect. When you joined, we made this very clear.

💛 Any deeper connection is something that may grow naturally over time — it is never something we can promise or arrange.

✋ Consent

Consent means someone has clearly said yesThis applies to everything — hugging, touching, private messaging, sharing photos, meeting up alone, and anything physical or romantic. A clear "yes" must come freely, without pressure, and can be taken back at any time.

Silence is not consentIf someone doesn't say no, that does not mean yes. If someone seems unsure, freezes, goes quiet, or doesn't respond clearly — that is not a yes either. Always check, and always wait for a clear yes.

A previous yes is not a yes foreverSomeone can change their mind at any time, even partway through something, even if they agreed before. If they say stop, you stop — straight away, every time.

Never pressure someone for a yesAsking again and again, guilt-tripping, sulking, or making someone feel bad for saying no is still a form of pressure — even if you don't mean it that way. Most people find pressure really uncomfortable, even when it feels small to the person doing it. A real yes is one that's given freely, not one that someone gave just to make the pressure stop.

⚠️ If consent is not respected

Not listening to someone's "no," or continuing after someone has asked you to stop, is something we take very seriously at Safe Soulmates.

This can lead to a member being removed from the community, and depending on what has happened, it may also need to be reported to the police. We have a duty to keep everyone safe, and that always comes first.

💛 If you are ever unsure about consent, or something has happened that didn't feel right, please talk to Vicky, Christine, or Amanda. You will be listened to, believed, and supported.

🧰 Tools to help you navigate this

You are in control. Here are some things you can do if you have feelings for someone, or if someone has feelings for you.

💬 Talk to staff

You can always speak to Vicky, Christine, Amanda, or any member of staff. They will listen without judgement and help you work out what to do.

🛑 You can say no

"No thank you" is a complete sentence. You do not have to explain why, make excuses, or feel guilty. Saying no to a meet up, a chat, or anything else is always OK — your boundaries are respected here, no matter what.

🚫 If someone doesn't stop

No always means no. Sometimes, because of someone's own needs, they may find it harder to recognise or process this straight away — but that never makes it OK to continue. If you've said no to something (like private messages) and they keep going, it is OK to block them. Please also let Vicky, Christine, or Amanda know.

⏳ Take your time

There is no rush. Friendships can grow slowly. You never have to make a decision quickly or feel pressured into anything.

📝 Think it through

Not sure how you feel? Write it down, talk it through with someone you trust, or ask staff to help you find the right words.

💚 Focus on friendship

Getting to know someone as a friend first is always the safest and kindest approach — for you and for them.

🙋 Ask for support

If someone's feelings are making you uncomfortable, tell a member of staff straight away. You will not get into trouble for speaking up.

RememberIf you have strong feelings for someone, or someone is making you feel uncomfortable, please speak to Vicky, Christine, Amanda, or any other member of staff. We are here to help — not to judge.

🎉 Events and meet ups

✔

Come as you are — events are relaxed and welcoming

✔

You do not have to talk to everyone or stay the whole time

✔

Always tell someone trusted where you are going

✔

Meet new people for the first time in public places

Pay for your own thingsThis saves you worrying about bills or feeling pressure to pay for others. For most meet ups, you can simply drink water and bring your own food — you don't need to spend a penny.

Bring whatever helps you feel comfortableFidgets, support animals, toys — anything that helps. Take breaks whenever you need to. We are a neurodivergent-friendly space, and that means doing what works for you.

🌈 If you feel a bit uncomfortable

We meet lots of different people — all lovely, but everyone has their own personality, and we embrace that. We want everyone to feel comfortable, but we also ask for patience with one another.

Our community is wonderfully diverse, with all kinds of communication styles and support needs. Please be patient — with others, and with yourself.

✔

Support workers and carers are always welcome

📱 WhatsApp groups

Messages between 9am and 9pm onlyLate messages can cause anxiety and disturb sleep. Please respect quiet time.

🌅 There are specific groups for early risers and night owls/late talkers — if you're in one of these, the usual times don't apply there, that's what they're for!

No "Good Morning" or "Good Night" messagesThese create lots of notifications and can feel overwhelming for many people.

You do not need to read everythingIt is OK to dip in and out. No pressure to keep up.

You can mute the group anytimeTaking breaks from your phone is healthy and encouraged.

No private messages without permissionOnly message someone privately if they have clearly said yes. If unwanted messages continue, tell any member of staff.

Only share kind, safe, suitable contentThink before sharing photos, videos, or links.

🛡️ Keeping yourself safe

Protect your personal informationDo not share your home address, bank details, PIN, or passwords with anyone.

Watch out for scamsSome people pretend to be friends to steal money or information. Report scams to Action Fraud: 0300 123 2040

Think before sharing photos of yourselfOnce shared, photos can be hard to take back. Real friends always respect your decisions.

Keep your phone chargedIn an emergency, 999 is always free to call.

Never leave your drink unattendedThis keeps you safe at events and in the community.

🛑 Respect — the stop rule

If someone says stop — you must stop immediatelyIt does not matter if you think something is funny or harmless. What matters is how it feels to the other person. Not stopping could mean being removed from the group.

What is triggering?Being triggered means something causes a sudden, strong emotional reaction — like panic, fear, or sadness. It is connected to past difficult experiences and is a real response. It is never "being dramatic." A word, joke, or topic can bring difficult feelings back without warning — even if you had no idea.

Not everyone shows feelings the same waySome people find it hard to notice or describe what they're feeling in the moment — sometimes called alexithymia. Someone might seem calm but actually be struggling, or might need extra time to work out how something has made them feel. This is just as real and valid as any other reaction, even if it doesn't show on the outside straight away.

How to respond when someone says stopStop straight away. Say "sorry, I didn't realise" if you can. Do not argue or say "it was just a joke." Move on kindly. Talk to any member of staff if you are unsure.
